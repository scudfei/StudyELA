# Alberta ELA Terminology Study Guide

Created: 2026-05-24

Source note:

- This document was separated from the Alberta ELA required-term source list in this folder.
- The terminology scope is limited to terms that already appear in the local Alberta ELA materials under `Results`.
- The examples are instructional examples for learning, identification, and multiple-choice reasoning. They are not quotations from the source materials.

## How To Use This Guide

1. Identify what the question is asking: definition, function, effect, theme, character change, or local evidence.
2. Break the term into necessary conditions. For example, `personification` requires a non-human thing receiving a human quality; `dynamic character` requires meaningful change.
3. Return to the text for evidence. If there is no textual anchor, the answer should not be treated as high confidence.

## 1. Text Understanding And Exam Task Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Context of Creation | The historical, social, cultural, or authorial situation in which a text was produced. | A poem written during wartime may use ruins to suggest loss and trauma. | Use it to explain the writer's world, not the reader's personal reaction. |
| Context of Reading | The reader's own time, knowledge, and values that shape interpretation. | A modern reader may notice unfair gender roles that an older text treats as normal. | This explains how readers understand a text, not how the text was originally created. |
| Writer's Purpose | The writer's reason for writing. Common purposes include to inform, persuade, entertain, describe, or explain. | An editorial using statistics and emotional language to call for a law change is persuasive. | Look for the action: inform gives information; persuade changes belief or action; entertain gives enjoyment; describe creates a picture. |
| Target Audience | The readers or listeners the writer seems to address. | A children's text uses simpler vocabulary and direct conflict. | Check vocabulary level, assumed background knowledge, formality, and direct address. |
| Explicit Purpose | A purpose stated directly in the text. | "This article explains how glaciers form." | The purpose is openly stated; little inference is needed. |
| Implicit Purpose | A purpose that must be inferred from tone, emphasis, structure, or selection of details. | A story about shopping may implicitly criticize vanity. | Look for repeated emphasis, irony, ending shifts, and what the text seems to approve or criticize. |
| Tone | The writer's or speaker's attitude toward the subject. | "What a brilliant plan," said after the plan fails, has a mocking tone. | Ask: how does the speaker feel about the subject? Use adjectives, adverbs, irony, and sentence structure. |
| Mood | The feeling or atmosphere created for the reader. | An empty house, cold rain, and dim light create a gloomy mood. | Tone is the speaker's attitude; mood is the reader's feeling. |
| Form | The type or structure of the text. | sonnet, letter, essay, play. | First identify what kind of text it is, then ask how that form shapes meaning. |
| Organizational Pattern | The way information is arranged. | cause/effect, compare/contrast, chronological order. | Look for transition words and paragraph movement. |
| Cause / Effect | A structure showing why something happens and what results from it. | Because the road flooded, the town was isolated. | For why/result/consequence questions, trace the event chain. |
| Compare / Contrast | A structure that shows similarities or differences. | City life is compared with rural life. | Look for similarly, unlike, whereas, however, but. |
| Chronological Order | Organization by time sequence. | childhood -> school -> adulthood. | Look for dates, then, after, next, finally. |
| Text Features | Non-body-text elements that help meaning. | title, subtitle, graph, caption, footnote, boldface, italics. | Do not read only the main text; exam questions may use charts, titles, notes, and layout. |
| Point of View Limits | What a narrator can and cannot know. | A limited narrator can enter only one character's mind. | Do not treat one character's view as objective truth unless the point of view supports it. |
| Creating Pathos | Creating emotional appeal. | Repeating "alone" can build sympathy. | Look for imagery, repetition, parallelism, and concrete suffering. |
| Controlling Idea | The central idea that controls the whole text. | Fear of the unknown can distort judgment. | It is not a single topic word; it should explain the main evidence across the text. |

## 2. Narrative And Plot Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrative | A text that tells events. | A story in which a character faces conflict and changes. | Look for characters, events, time movement, and conflict. |
| Narrator | The voice or figure who tells the story. | "I walked into the room." | The narrator is not automatically the author. |
| Narrative Point of View | The perspective and knowledge range from which the story is told. | first person, third-person limited, omniscient. | Check pronouns and what the narrator knows. |
| First-person Narrator | A narrator who uses I or we. | "I could not understand why she left." | The narrator knows personal experience directly but may be biased or unreliable. |
| Third-person Narrator | A narrator who uses he, she, or they. | "She waited by the window." | Then determine whether the narration is omniscient, limited, or objective. |
| Omniscient Narrator | A narrator who can enter several minds and know a broad context. | The narration knows both the mother's fear and the son's shame. | More than one inner life is available to the reader. |
| Limited-omniscient Narrator | A third-person narrator limited mainly to one character's mind. | The narration knows Anita's thoughts, while others are shown through speech and action. | Ask whether the narration follows one consciousness. |
| Objective Narrator | A narrator who reports only observable action and speech. | "He closed the door. She looked away." | Camera-like narration; no direct access to thoughts or feelings. |
| Plot | The arrangement of events. | A misunderstanding begins, conflict rises, truth is revealed. | Plot is not just events; it includes order and cause. |
| Plot Diagram | A model of plot structure. | exposition -> rising action -> climax -> resolution. | Use it to identify the function of a moment in the story. |
| Exposition | Opening background information. | Characters, setting, and basic situation are introduced. | Usually appears near the beginning. |
| Antecedent Action | Events that happened before the story begins but affect the present action. | An old family feud causes the current conflict. | Often revealed through memory, explanation, or dialogue. |
| Complication | A new problem that makes the situation more difficult. | The character starts a journey and discovers the map is wrong. | It blocks or complicates the original goal. |
| Rising Action | The series of events that intensifies the conflict. | Each attempt to solve the problem creates more pressure. | It moves toward the climax. |
| Conflict | The struggle that drives the story. | person vs person, society, nature, self. | Ask: what does the character want, and what blocks it? |
| Climax | The highest point of conflict or the turning point. | A character must make a decisive choice. | Usually the moment of greatest pressure, risk, or revelation. |
| Resolution | The outcome or settling of the main conflict. | The truth is revealed and the characters face the result. | It does not have to be happy; the main conflict has an outcome. |
| Denouement | Final tying-up after the main resolution. | The story explains what happened after the crisis. | More like aftermath or closing explanation. |
| Subplot | A secondary plot line. | The main plot is war; the subplot is two brothers reconciling. | It supports, mirrors, or contrasts the main plot. |
| Setting | Time, place, and social environment. | a winter forest, a small prairie town. | For effect questions, ask how the setting shapes mood, conflict, or character. |
| Atmosphere | The overall feeling created by a setting or scene. | An abandoned house creates a suspenseful atmosphere. | Close to mood, but often tied to environment. |
| Theme | A central insight about life, society, or human nature. | Pride can destroy judgment. | Not a single word; it is usually a full general statement. |
| Moral | A direct lesson or rule. | Do not lie. | A moral is more lesson-like; a theme can be more complex. |

## 3. Character And Characterization Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Characterization | The methods used to create a character. | action, speech, appearance, thoughts, others' comments. | Ask how the author makes us understand the character. |
| Direct Characterization | The text directly states a trait. | "Maria was generous." | The trait is named directly. |
| Indirect Characterization | The reader infers a trait from evidence. | Maria secretly pays a stranger's bus fare. | Use behaviour, speech, thoughts, and reactions as evidence. |
| Character Development | How a character is revealed or changes over the text. | A timid character gradually learns responsibility. | Compare the character at the beginning and end. |
| Character Motivation | The reason behind a character's action. | A character refuses to compete because he fears failure. | Test it with "the character does X because Y." |
| Personality | A character's stable traits. | proud, timid, generous. | Requires repeated evidence, not one isolated action. |
| Appearance | Physical description. | a worn coat, polished shoes. | Appearance may suggest class, mood, or condition, but needs textual support. |
| Flat Character | A simple character with few traits and little complexity. | A minor character who only shows greed. | Few traits; low complexity. |
| Round Character | A complex character with several traits or contradictions. | Brave but selfish; loving but evasive. | Multiple traits can explain the character. |
| Stock Character | A familiar, conventional character type. | greedy landlord, wise mentor. | Recognizable type; limited individuality. |
| Archetypal Character / Archetype | A widely recurring character pattern across cultures. | hero, trickster, mentor. | More universal and symbolic than a stock character. |
| Dynamic Character | A character who changes meaningfully. | A character realizes her vanity and changes her choices. | Must show a change in worldview, attitude, values, or behaviour. |
| Static Character | A character who remains essentially unchanged. | A character keeps the same prejudice after major events. | Events happen, but the core character does not change. |
| Foil | A character used to contrast with another character. | A brave friend highlights the protagonist's fear. | Similar situation plus contrasting traits. |
| Protagonist | The central character or main action focus. | The story follows her choices and consequences. | Not necessarily a good person. |
| Antagonist | A character or force that opposes the protagonist. | rival, law, storm, inner fear. | Not necessarily evil; the key is opposition. |
| Major Character | An important character who affects the main plot or theme. | A character who appears often and changes the outcome. | Important to plot or theme. |
| Minor Character | A less central character with a limited function. | A messenger who moves the plot forward. | Usually serves the protagonist, plot, or theme. |

## 4. Poetry Form, Meter, And Genre Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Poem | A compressed literary text that often emphasizes sound, rhythm, image, and line breaks. | a sonnet or a free verse poem. | Do not rely only on rhyme; notice lineation and image density. |
| Poetry | The broad literary category of poems. | lyric poetry, narrative poetry. | Often uses concentrated language, sound, and imagery. |
| Poetic Technique | A method used in poetry. | enjambment, imagery, rhyme. | A technique is how the poem is made. |
| Poetic Device | A specific literary or sound device in poetry. | metaphor, alliteration, symbol. | Often tested by effect or function questions. |
| Line | One line of a poem. | Each horizontal unit of verse. | Line breaks can create pause, emphasis, or surprise. |
| Stanza | A group of lines in a poem. | a four-line stanza. | Works like a paragraph in poetry. |
| Verse | A general term for poetic line, stanza, or poetic form depending on context. | blank verse. | Use context to decide whether it means line, stanza, or form. |
| Rhyme | Similarity of sound, usually at word endings. | light / night. | Listen to sound, not only spelling. |
| Internal Rhyme | Rhyme within a line. | A line containing two words with matching sounds. | The rhyming words occur inside the same line. |
| External Rhyme | Rhyme between lines. | The end of line 1 rhymes with the end of line 2. | Often overlaps with end rhyme. |
| End Rhyme | Rhyme at line endings. | sky / high at the ends of two lines. | Main basis for rhyme scheme. |
| Rhyme Scheme | The pattern of end rhyme. | ABAB, AABB. | Assign letters to matching end sounds. |
| Rhythm | The movement or beat of language. | Short quick lines can create urgency. | Created by stress, pause, repetition, and sentence length. |
| Meter / Metre | A regular pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables. | iambic pentameter. | Count both stress pattern and number of feet. |
| Foot | A unit of meter. | iamb, trochee. | Divide the line into rhythmic units. |
| Iambic Foot | An unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable. | to-DAY. | Pattern: da-DUM. |
| Trochaic Foot | A stressed syllable followed by an unstressed syllable. | TA-ble. | Pattern: DUM-da. |
| Iambic Pentameter | A line with five iambic feet. | da-DUM repeated five times. | `iambic` names the stress pattern; `pentameter` means five feet. |
| Scansion | Marking the meter of a poem. | Marking syllables as unstressed or stressed. | Read naturally, mark stresses, then divide into feet. |
| Metrical Poetry / Traditional Poetry | Poetry with regular meter and often regular rhyme. | Many sonnets use regular meter. | Distinguished from free verse by stable rhythm or rhyme pattern. |
| Free Verse | Poetry without fixed meter or fixed rhyme scheme, though it still has rhythm or cadence. | Irregular line lengths with no clear end rhyme. | Not "no technique"; look for cadence, line breaks, and imagery. |
| Cadence | The natural rise and fall of language. | Speech-like rhythm in free verse. | Useful for discussing rhythm without fixed meter. |
| Blank Verse | Unrhymed iambic pentameter. | Dramatic monologue in unrhymed five-beat lines. | `blank` means no rhyme; meter is still present. |
| End-stopped Line | A line that ends with a grammatical or meaningful pause. | "The sun has set." | The line ending feels complete. |
| Enjambment | A sentence or phrase continues across a line break. | "I walked into / the dark room." | The line does not pause at the end. |
| Sonnet | A fourteen-line poem, often with a set structure and turn. | Shakespearean sonnet. | Check fourteen lines, rhyme pattern, turn, and final couplet. |
| Shakespearean / Elizabethan Sonnet | A sonnet often structured as three quatrains and a final couplet. | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. | The final couplet often summarizes, turns, or intensifies the idea. |
| Quatrain | A four-line stanza or unit. | One four-line section of a sonnet. | `quad` means four. |
| Couplet | Two lines, often rhymed, forming a unit. | Two final rhyming lines of a sonnet. | A final couplet often concludes, summarizes, or reverses. |
| Lyric | A poem focused on personal feeling or reflection. | A speaker expresses grief, love, or awe. | Usually not driven by a full plot. |
| Ode | A formal poem of praise. | an ode to autumn. | Elevated tone and praise are central. |
| Elegy | A poem of mourning or loss. | a poem grieving a death. | Look for grief, mourning, memory, and loss. |
| Ballad | A song-like narrative poem. | A poem telling a tragic love story. | Often narrative, rhythmic, and repetitive. |
| Epic | A long narrative poem about heroic or culturally significant events. | a hero's large-scale journey. | Broad scope, public importance, heroic action. |

## 5. Sound Device Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sound Device / Aural Device | A device that creates meaning or effect through sound. | rhyme, alliteration, onomatopoeia. | First hear the repeated sound, then ask what effect it creates. |
| Alliteration | Repetition of initial consonant sounds in nearby words. | wild wind whipped. | Focus on starting consonant sounds, not spelling alone. |
| Assonance | Repetition of vowel sounds. | bright sky repeats a long i sound. | Vowel sound repeats; end rhyme is not required. |
| Consonance | Repetition of consonant sounds, not necessarily at word beginnings. | blank / think share the nk sound. | Unlike alliteration, it can occur anywhere in the word. |
| Onomatopoeia | A word whose sound imitates a real sound. | buzz, clang, crash. | The word should sound like the thing it names. |
| Repetition | Repeated words, phrases, sounds, structures, or images. | "Never, never, never." | Ask what is emphasized and what rhythm or obsession is created. |
| Half Rhyme / Near Rhyme | Imperfect or partial rhyme. | shape / keep may share only partial sound. | Similar but not full rhyme; often creates instability or tension. |

## 6. Imagery, Symbol, Allusion, And Figurative Language Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Figurative Language | Non-literal language that creates meaning through comparison, exaggeration, substitution, or symbol. | "Her words were knives." | Do not read it literally; ask what is being connected. |
| Figure of Speech | A specific type of figurative expression. | simile, metaphor, hyperbole. | Identify the form first, then its effect. |
| Imagery | Sensory language that appeals to sight, sound, touch, smell, or taste. | cold blue light. | Look for concrete sensory detail, not abstract idea alone. |
| Symbol | A concrete thing in the text that stands for an abstract idea. | a locked door representing isolation. | Must be a specific textual object carrying larger meaning. |
| Symbolism | The use or pattern of symbols. | winter repeatedly suggesting death or stagnation. | Look for repeated objects connected to theme. |
| Motif | A recurring element, image, action, or phrase. | repeated mirrors in a story. | A motif may support a theme without being a full symbol. |
| Reference | A general mention of another person, event, text, or body of knowledge. | a mention of a famous battle. | Broader than allusion; not every reference is deeply meaningful. |
| Allusion | A meaningful reference to literature, religion, history, myth, or culture outside the text. | "He met his Waterloo." | Identify the external source and what meaning it adds. |
| Metaphor | A direct comparison that states or implies A is B. | Time is a thief. | No like/as; identify the shared quality. |
| Simile | A comparison using like or as. | The wind moved like a cripple. | Has like/as; the answer should explain the comparison point. |
| Personification | Giving human qualities to non-human things. | The anxious wave reached for the boat. | Non-human subject plus human feeling, action, or intention. |
| Apostrophe | Direct address to an absent person, abstract idea, or non-human thing. | "O Death, where is your sting?" | The speaker directly addresses something not present as an ordinary listener. |
| Hyperbole | Deliberate exaggeration. | I waited a thousand years. | Literally impossible or excessive, used for emphasis. |
| Understatement | Deliberately making something seem less important or intense than it is. | After a storm: "It was a bit windy." | The opposite of hyperbole; often ironic. |
| Oxymoron | Contradictory words placed together in a phrase. | bittersweet, deafening silence. | Usually a short phrase with opposing terms. |
| Paradox | A statement that seems contradictory but may reveal truth. | Less is more. | Usually a whole statement or idea, not just a two-word phrase. |
| Allegory | A narrative in which characters and events systematically represent abstract ideas. | A journey representing spiritual growth. | More than one symbol; the whole story maps onto another meaning system. |
| Fable | A short moral story, often with animals or simple characters. | a story teaching patience through a race. | Usually brief, simple, and moral-centered. |
| Synecdoche | A part stands for the whole, or the whole stands for a part. | "all hands on deck" uses hands for sailors. | The key relationship is part-whole. |
| Metonymy | A related object or idea stands for another thing. | "the Crown" for monarchy or government. | The relationship is association, not part-whole. |
| Idiom | A fixed expression whose meaning is not predictable from its literal words. | kick the bucket = die. | Literal reading does not work; recognize the fixed expression. |

Quick distinctions:

- `simile` uses like/as; `metaphor` directly equates or implies A as B.
- `symbol` is an object inside the text carrying abstract meaning; `allusion` points outside the text.
- `synecdoche` uses a part-whole relationship; `metonymy` uses association.
- `oxymoron` is usually a contradictory phrase; `paradox` is usually a contradictory-looking statement or idea.
- `hyperbole` exaggerates; `understatement` minimizes.

## 7. Irony, Satire, Ambiguity, And Critical Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Irony | A gap between surface meaning, expectation, or knowledge and the deeper reality. | A fire station burns down. | Ask: what is expected or said, what actually happens, and where is the gap? |
| Verbal Irony | A statement whose intended meaning differs from its literal wording. | In heavy rain: "Lovely weather." | Depends on context and speaker attitude. |
| Situational Irony | An event turns out differently from what is reasonably expected. | A security expert's house is robbed. | The irony is in the outcome, not just in a sentence. |
| Dramatic Irony | The audience or reader knows something a character does not. | The reader knows a letter is false, but the character believes it. | The knowledge gap is between audience and character. |
| Tragic Irony | Dramatic irony in a tragic situation, often leading toward disaster. | A character tries to avoid danger but moves closer to it. | Look for ignorance, fate, and serious consequence. |
| Sarcasm | Sharp or cutting verbal irony. | To someone very late: "So punctual." | More attacking or mocking than general verbal irony. |
| Satire | Humour, exaggeration, irony, or ridicule used to criticize society, institutions, or human behaviour. | A corrupt official is portrayed in absurd terms. | Identify the target: who or what is being criticized? |
| Ambiguity | More than one possible meaning. | "I saw her duck" can mean an action or an animal. | Check whether two or more interpretations are genuinely possible. |
| Incongruity | A mismatch or lack of fit. | A formal funeral interrupted by an absurd advertising slogan. | Often creates humour, irony, or satire. |
| Contradiction | Two claims, actions, or facts that cannot both be true. | A character praises honesty while constantly lying. | Can support irony, hypocrisy, or conflict. |
| Rationalization | A false or weak excuse made to seem reasonable. | "I cheated only because I did not want to disappoint my parents." | The surface reason sounds acceptable, but it covers a problem. |
| Hypocrisy | A conflict between stated values and actual behaviour. | A public critic of greed secretly takes bribes. | Compare professed belief with action. |

## 8. Syntax And Rhetorical Structure Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sentence Structure | The design of sentences, including length, fragments, order, and completeness. | A run of short sentences can create speed or tension. | For effect questions, ask how form changes pace, emphasis, or feeling. |
| Repetition | Repeated words, sounds, structures, or images. | "We wait. We listen. We hope." | Identify what repeats and what that repetition emphasizes. |
| Anaphora | Repetition at the beginning of successive clauses, lines, or sentences. | "I have a dream..." repeated at the start of clauses. | A specific type of repetition at the beginning. |
| Parallelism | Repetition of grammatical structure. | to see clearly, to speak honestly, to act bravely. | Same structure creates balance, emphasis, or persuasive force. |
| Rhetorical Question | A question asked for effect rather than to get an answer. | How could anyone ignore this? | Ask what it reveals or emphasizes, not just what the literal answer is. |
| Word Placement | The position of a word affects emphasis or meaning. | Ending a sentence with "alone" emphasizes isolation. | Notice beginnings, endings, isolated lines, and words after dashes. |
| Sentence Order | The sequence in which sentences present information. | Giving the result first, then explaining the cause. | Ask how the order controls suspense, emphasis, or interpretation. |

## 9. Text Form And Genre Terms

| Term | Meaning | Example | How to identify or distinguish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Expository / Exposition | Writing that explains information or ideas. | An article explaining how glaciers form. | Main purpose is to clarify, inform, or explain. |
| Persuasive / Persuasion | Writing that tries to change belief or action. | An editorial calling for a law change. | Look for claim, reasons, evidence, and emotional appeal. |
| Narrative / Narration | Writing that tells events. | A personal memory of a trip. | Contains time movement and events. |
| Descriptive / Description | Writing that presents a person, place, object, or scene through detail. | A description of a street during a storm. | Focuses on sensory details; may not have a full plot. |
| Short Story | A brief fictional narrative. | A story with one central conflict and a turning point. | Usually concentrated in character, setting, and conflict. |
| Novella | A work longer than a short story but shorter than a novel. | A focused but extended fictional narrative. | More developed than a short story, less expansive than a novel. |
| Novel | A long fictional narrative. | A multi-character story with several plot lines. | Broader development of character, society, and conflict. |
| Epistolary Novel | A novel told through letters, diaries, emails, or documents. | A story built from letters between characters. | Form limits what information is available and shapes point of view. |
| Play | A dramatic text intended for performance. | Dialogue plus stage directions. | Meaning comes through speech, action, and staging. |
| Screenplay | A script for film or television. | Scene headings, action lines, and dialogue. | Read visual action and camera-friendly detail. |
| Motion Picture | A film text. | A movie using image, sound, and editing. | Analyze visual and audio choices as well as dialogue. |
| Fiction | Invented or imagined narrative. | short story, novel. | Does not primarily claim factual record. |
| Non-fiction | Writing based on real people, events, or ideas. | essay, memoir, biography. | Still shaped by selection, structure, tone, and purpose. |
| Biography | A life story written by someone else. | A book about a scientist's life. | Third person writes about another person. |
| Autobiography | A life story written by the subject. | A writer tells the story of their own life. | Self-written and usually broad in life scope. |
| Memoir | A focused account of part of the writer's life. | A writer reflects on childhood immigration. | More selective and theme-focused than autobiography. |
| Essay | A prose text developing an idea, argument, reflection, or experience. | personal essay, critical essay. | Notice voice, idea development, and reflection. |
| Letter to the Editor | A public letter responding to an issue. | A reader argues against a city policy. | Has a clear audience and persuasive purpose. |
| Graphic Text | A text that communicates through images, layout, and words. | political cartoon, poster, infographic. | Analyze words, images, symbols, layout, and visual emphasis together. |
