# Literary Terms Teaching Guide: Five Groups of Commonly Confused Concepts

*All examples have been verified against canonical literary works and standard educational references.*
*Reading the original texts alongside this guide is recommended.*

---

## Part 1: Three Types of Irony

Irony is a broad term for moments when the literal meaning of language or the surface outcome of events diverges from the actual meaning or expected outcome. The three subtypes differ in the nature of that gap — and exams require you to identify which type applies precisely.

---

### 1.1 Verbal Irony

**Definition:**
The speaker (or narrator) says something whose literal meaning is the opposite of what they actually intend. The reader perceives the gap between what is said and what is meant.

Distinction from sarcasm: Sarcasm is a form of verbal irony, but it carries a specific aggressive or mocking intent directed at a target. Verbal irony can be more neutral in tone.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Shakespeare, *Julius Caesar***
Mark Antony's funeral oration repeats the phrase:
> "For Brutus is an honourable man."

Antony catalogues Caesar's virtues and popular support, and each return to "Brutus is an honourable man" makes the opposite meaning clearer: Brutus betrayed Caesar and is anything but honourable. The words are praise; the intent is accusation. This is the textbook definition of verbal irony.

**Example 2 — Jonathan Swift, *A Modest Proposal* (1729)**
In a calm, academic register, the narrator proposes selling Irish infants as food to solve the problem of poverty:
> "a young healthy child well nursed is at a year old a most delicious, nourishing, and wholesome food."

Swift's actual target is British indifference to Irish suffering. The proposal is monstrous; the tone is reasonable and measured. That sustained gap between register and content is sustained verbal irony across the entire essay.

**Example 3 — Jane Austen, *Pride and Prejudice* (1813)**
The novel's opening line:
> "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."

The narrator presents this as an established fact. What is actually true in this social world is the reverse: it is families with unmarried daughters who are desperately seeking wealthy husbands. The narrator states an inverted proposition with total authority, inviting the reader to notice the gap.

**How to identify:**
1. Find the literal meaning of what the speaker or narrator says.
2. Ask: in this context, is that literal meaning absurd, logically impossible, or clearly contradicted by the facts?
3. Yes → verbal irony.
4. Additional check: is the speaker deliberately mocking a specific target? Yes → also sarcasm.

---

### 1.2 Situational Irony

**Definition:**
The actual outcome of events is the opposite of what the characters (or the reader) expected. Something that should have produced one result produces the reverse.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — O. Henry, *The Gift of the Magi* (1905)**
Della sells her hair to buy Jim a chain for his gold watch. Jim sells his gold watch to buy Della a set of combs for her hair. Both gifts are rendered useless at the moment of exchange — by the very act of sacrifice that made them possible. This is the most frequently cited example of situational irony in short fiction.

**Example 2 — Sophocles, *Oedipus Rex* (c. 429 BCE)**
Oedipus learns of the prophecy that he will kill his father and marry his mother. To prevent it, he flees the city of Corinth — not knowing that he was adopted, and that his biological parents are in Thebes. His act of escape is the very mechanism that brings the prophecy to completion. The attempt to avoid fate becomes the means of fulfilling it.

**Example 3 — Saki, *The Interlopers***
Bitter enemies Ulrich and Georg are pinned under a fallen tree. In their shared helplessness they resolve their feud and call out together for rescue. The last line:
> "Are they your men?" asked Georg. / "No," said Ulrich with a laugh — "Wolves."

The rescuers they have been calling for turn out to be predators. Their reconciliation, which seemed to be a kind of salvation, arrives just in time to make no difference.

**How to identify:**
1. Ask: what did the characters (or the reader) expect to happen?
2. Ask: what actually happened?
3. Are the two outcomes opposite in direction, and is the reversal meaningful rather than random?
4. Yes → situational irony.

---

### 1.3 Dramatic Irony

**Definition:**
The reader (or audience) possesses information that one or more characters in the story do not. This information asymmetry creates tension, dread, or tragedy — because we watch characters act on incomplete knowledge.

Note: dramatic irony does not require anything to be "said the wrong way around" (that is verbal irony's defining feature). Its core is the **gap between what the reader knows and what the character knows**.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Shakespeare, *Romeo and Juliet***
Juliet has taken a sleeping potion and appears dead. A letter explaining the plan never reaches Romeo. He arrives at her tomb, sees her apparently dead, and drinks poison. The audience knows she is alive. Watching Romeo walk toward an unnecessary death — unable to intervene — is the defining experience of dramatic irony in Western theatre.

**Example 2 — Shakespeare, *Macbeth*, Act I, Scene vi**
King Duncan arrives at Macbeth's castle and says:
> "This castle hath a pleasant seat; the air / Nimbly and sweetly recommends itself / Unto our gentle senses."

The audience has just witnessed Macbeth and Lady Macbeth plan the king's murder. Duncan is admiring the home where he is about to be killed. His words of pleasure are in direct tension with what the audience knows.

**Example 3 — Sophocles, *Oedipus Rex***
Oedipus swears to find and punish the murderer of the former king, Laius. The audience — either through prior knowledge of the myth or the prophet Tiresias's warnings — knows that the murderer is Oedipus himself. The entire investigation is a man methodically uncovering his own destruction.

**How to identify:**
1. As the reader, do I know something important that a character in the story does not?
2. Does that information gap shape how I understand the character's actions or words?
3. Yes → dramatic irony.
4. Key distinction: dramatic irony = **reader knows more than the character**; situational irony = **outcome contradicts expectation**. Both can coexist (as in *Romeo and Juliet*), but they are not the same thing.

## Part 2: Four Types of Narrative Point of View

Point of view determines who is telling the story and how much information the reader has access to. Exams frequently require you to distinguish between closely related types — especially omniscient versus limited omniscient.

---

### Comparison Table

| Type | Person | Access to characters' inner lives | How many characters |
|------|--------|-----------------------------------|---------------------|
| First-person | I / we | Narrator's own thoughts only | 1 (the narrator) |
| Third-person Limited | He / she / they | Yes | Only 1 character |
| Third-person Omniscient | He / she / they | Yes | Any number |
| Third-person Objective | He / she / they | None | 0 |

---

### 2.1 First-person Narration

**Definition:** The narrator speaks as "I" and tells the story from their own perspective. The reader has direct access only to the narrator's thoughts and perceptions. Other characters' inner lives are accessible only when the narrator guesses at them or is told directly.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Charles Dickens, *Great Expectations* (1861)**
> "My father's family name being Pirrip, and my Christian name Philip, my infant tongue could make of both names nothing longer or more explicit than Pip."

Pip narrates his own childhood from an adult vantage point. The reader's understanding of all other characters — Miss Havisham, Magwitch, Estella — depends entirely on Pip's observations and interpretations.

**Example 2 — Harper Lee, *To Kill a Mockingbird* (1960)**
Scout narrates her childhood years in an adult voice. Everything the reader sees is filtered through her perspective: her understanding of her father, her perception of racial injustice, her reading of adult motivations. We see what Scout sees, and no more.

**Example 3 — Charlotte Brontë, *Jane Eyre* (1847)**
> "There was no possibility of taking a walk that day."

Jane narrates her full journey from orphan to independent woman in first person. Rochester's inner life, and the true state of his marriage, are available to the reader only through what Jane observes, suspects, or is told — which is exactly what makes the novel's central mystery sustainable.

**How to identify:**
- Narrator uses "I / me / my / we."
- Narrator can only guess at or infer other characters' thoughts — never state them directly.

---

### 2.2 Third-person Limited Omniscient

**Definition:** The story is told in the third person (he/she), but the narrator has direct access to the inner life of **one** specific character only. The reader sees the story through that character's thoughts and perceptions; other characters' inner lives are not directly revealed.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — J.K. Rowling, *Harry Potter* series (1997–2007)**
The series is narrated in third person, but the perspective almost never leaves Harry. The reader has direct access to Harry's thoughts, feelings, and inferences. What Dumbledore or Voldemort are actually thinking is known to the reader only when they speak or when Harry reasons it out. Many of the series' central mysteries — Snape's true allegiance, for instance — are sustained precisely because the narrator is locked to Harry's limited view.

**Example 2 — Lois Lowry, *The Giver* (1993)**
> "It was almost December, and Jonas was beginning to be frightened."

Third-person narration, entirely following Jonas's perspective and experience. The reader comes to understand the dystopian society only through Jonas's growing awareness. What the Giver, the Elders, or Jonas's parents truly think is not directly presented.

**How to identify:**
- Third-person narration (he/she).
- The narrator can describe one character's thoughts and feelings directly.
- When other characters appear, their inner lives are **not** directly given.
- Test: pick a second important character — does the narrator ever say what that character is thinking? If not → third-person limited.

---

### 2.3 Third-person Omniscient

**Definition:** Third-person narration in which the narrator can enter any character's mind, move freely between perspectives, and even comment on events from a position beyond any single character's knowledge — a "god's-eye view."

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Leo Tolstoy, *War and Peace* (1869)**
The narrator moves freely among hundreds of characters across Russian society. The reader has direct access to Prince Andrei's dying consciousness, Natasha's emotional collapse, Napoleon's arrogance. No single character is the consistent "focus point." The narrator's view encompasses the full sweep of history and human experience.

**Example 2 — George Eliot, *Middlemarch* (1871–1872)**
The narrator writes in the manner of a social historian, entering the inner lives of Dorothea, Casaubon, Lydgate, and others in turn, while also making direct evaluative comments to the reader about human nature and social conditions. It is one of the most widely analyzed examples of omniscient narration in English literature.

**How to identify:**
- Third-person narration.
- Within a single chapter or scene, the narrator enters **multiple different characters'** minds.
- The narrator sometimes addresses the reader directly or comments from a position no single character occupies.

---

### 2.4 Third-person Objective

**Definition:** Third-person narration in which the narrator **does not enter any character's mind**. Only externally observable actions, dialogue, and setting are presented — like a camera recording a scene. The reader must infer what characters think and feel from what they say and do.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Ernest Hemingway, *Hills Like White Elephants* (1927)**
The story consists almost entirely of dialogue. The narrator never describes what either character is thinking. Two people discuss something that is never named (conventionally interpreted as an abortion). The reader cannot know with certainty what either character wants. All interpretation must come from word choice, tone, hesitation, and evasion. This is the defining example of Hemingway's "iceberg theory" in practice.

**Example 2 — Ernest Hemingway, *The Killers* (1927)**
Two hired killers arrive at a diner to wait for their target, Ole Andreson. The narrator reports what happens without explaining motive or feeling. Why Ole does not try to escape is never answered by the narrator — the reader is left to infer.

**How to identify:**
- Third-person narration.
- No instances of "he thought / she felt / he wondered" or any direct access to inner states.
- Information reaches the reader only through: dialogue, observable actions, and physical description of setting.
- Test: find a moment of high tension — does the narrator tell you what any character is thinking or feeling? No → third-person objective.

## Part 3: Two Sonnet Structures

The sonnet is a fixed form of 14 lines, but there are two principal structural types. They differ in where the **volta** (turn or pivot) occurs and how the rhyme scheme is organised.

---

### Comparison Table

| | Shakespearean Sonnet | Petrarchan Sonnet |
|---|---|---|
| Also called | English Sonnet | Italian Sonnet |
| Structure | Three quatrains + one couplet | Octave (8 lines) + sestet (6 lines) |
| Rhyme scheme | ABAB CDCD EFEF GG | ABBAABBA + CDECDE (or variations) |
| Volta position | Before the final couplet (line 13) | At line 9, the transition from octave to sestet |
| Closing effect | Couplet provides summary or reversal | Sestet provides a response or resolution |

---

### 3.1 Shakespearean Sonnet

**Definition:** Three independently developed quatrains (each following ABAB), culminating in a rhyming couplet (GG) that summarises, concludes, or reverses the preceding argument.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Shakespeare, *Sonnet 18***
> "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? / Thou art more lovely and more temperate..."

The three quatrains develop the comparison at different levels. The final couplet:
> "So long as men can breathe or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee."

The couplet closes the argument: the poem itself will preserve the beloved's beauty. Rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.

**Example 2 — Shakespeare, *Sonnet 73***
> "That time of year thou mayst in me behold / When yellow leaves, or none, or few, do hang..."

Three quatrains build three parallel images for old age — autumn trees, fading twilight, a dying fire — each making the same point with increasing intensity. Final couplet:
> "This thou perceiv'st, which makes thy love more strong, / To love that well which thou must leave ere long."

**Example 3 — Shakespeare, *Sonnet 60***
> "Like as the waves make towards the pebbled shore, / So do our minutes hasten to their end..."

This sonnet appears in the Solaro practice materials covered in this course. Students correctly identified the iambic rhythm; the rhyme scheme is standard ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.

**How to identify:**
1. Count the lines: 14.
2. Do the last two lines rhyme with each other? (couplet) → Yes → possibly Shakespearean.
3. Are the first 12 lines organised into three groups of four, each following ABAB? → Yes → Shakespearean confirmed.
4. The volta typically occurs just before the couplet; the couplet delivers the turn or conclusion.

---

### 3.2 Petrarchan Sonnet

**Definition:** An octave (eight lines) states a problem, describes a situation, or poses a question; a sestet (six lines) responds, resolves, or reflects. The volta falls at line 9 — the transition point. The octave typically rhymes ABBAABBA; the sestet has several accepted variants.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — John Milton, *On His Blindness* (c. 1655)**
> "When I consider how my light is spent / Ere half my days, in this dark world and wide..."

Octave: Milton, now blind, fears he cannot use his talent to serve God and worries God will expect the same from him as before. The volta at line 9:
> "God doth not need / Either man's work or his own gifts... / They also serve who only stand and wait."

The sestet shifts from anxious self-questioning to acceptance of God's will. The structural turn is unmistakable. This is one of the most frequently cited examples of the Petrarchan form in the English tradition.

**Example 2 — Elizabeth Barrett Browning, *Sonnet 43* (1850)**
> "How do I love thee? Let me count the ways. / I love thee to the depth and breadth and height..."

From *Sonnets from the Portuguese*, following Petrarchan structure. The octave enumerates love in earthly dimensions; the sestet elevates it to a spiritual and eternal plane.

**How to identify:**
1. Count the lines: 14.
2. Is there a clear shift in argument or tone between line 8 and line 9? → That is the volta.
3. Do the final two lines **not** rhyme as a couplet? → Not Shakespearean.
4. Do the first eight lines follow ABBAABBA? → Petrarchan confirmed.
5. Content shortcut: octave = problem or situation / sestet = response or resolution.

## Part 4: Four Types of Conflict

Conflict is the central engine of narrative. A single work can contain multiple types simultaneously, but usually one is dominant.

---

### 4.1 Man vs. Man

**Definition:** The protagonist is in direct opposition to another individual character. This is typically the surface-level conflict — the most immediately visible antagonism.

**Examples:**

1. **Saki, *The Interlopers*** — The generations-long feud between Ulrich von Gradwitz and Georg Znaeym is the story's driving conflict until the falling tree reverses the situation entirely.

2. **Shakespeare, *Romeo and Juliet*** — The Montague-Capulet feud is the man vs. man conflict that determines both protagonists' fates from the beginning.

3. **Harper Lee, *To Kill a Mockingbird*** — The conflict between Bob Ewell and Atticus Finch intensifies through the Tom Robinson trial and erupts in direct physical violence in the novel's final section.

**How to identify:**
- Who is blocking the protagonist? → If it is a specific individual person → Man vs. Man.

---

### 4.2 Man vs. Nature

**Definition:** The protagonist faces the threat of a natural environment, natural forces, or animals. This type of conflict typically emphasises the smallness and vulnerability of human beings against forces they cannot control.

**Examples:**

1. **Jack London, *To Build a Fire* (1908)** — A man travels alone through the Yukon wilderness in extreme cold, attempting to light a fire. His rational judgments fail one by one. He dies of exposure. Nature (temperature) is the absolute antagonist. This is the definitive example of Man vs. Nature in the short story canon.

2. **Ernest Hemingway, *The Old Man and the Sea* (1952)** — Santiago's battle with the marlin, followed by his sustained defensive fight against the sharks. The sea, the fish, and his own physical limits constitute the primary opposing force.

3. **Saki, *The Interlopers*** — After the two men reconcile, a falling beech tree traps them both; then wolves arrive. Nature intervenes passively and then lethally. Note: this story contains both Man vs. Man (the feud) and Man vs. Nature (the tree, the wolves) — one shifting into the other.

**How to identify:**
- The obstacle facing the protagonist is weather, terrain, animals, disease, or another natural force → Man vs. Nature.

---

### 4.3 Man vs. Self

**Definition:** The protagonist experiences an internal conflict — between moral demands and desire, between fear and action, between two aspects of identity. No external antagonist is required; the battlefield is inside the character.

**Examples:**

1. **Shakespeare, *Hamlet*** — "To be, or not to be, that is the question." Hamlet knows his father was murdered. He repeatedly delays acting on that knowledge. The central conflict of the play is internal: act or endure? Revenge or acceptance? Moral scrupulousness against grief and anger.

2. **Shakespeare, *Macbeth*** — Macbeth desires the throne but understands the moral weight of murdering a king who trusts him. His soliloquies, the hallucinated dagger, and his mental collapse after the murder all dramatise the internal conflict between ambition and conscience.

3. **Robert Louis Stevenson, *Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* (1886)** — Jekyll attempts to separate the good and evil halves of human nature by chemical means. He loses control of the Hyde side entirely. The novel is the most direct literary representation of Man vs. Self — the protagonist is literally divided against himself.

**How to identify:**
- The conflict takes place inside the protagonist; no external figure is the primary antagonist.
- Signal words: hesitation, doubt, guilt, moral struggle, identity crisis.

---

### 4.4 Man vs. Society

**Definition:** The protagonist is in opposition to a social system, collective norm, institutional structure, or cultural prejudice. The antagonist is not a single person but a collective force — a whole way of organising the world.

**Examples:**

1. **Harper Lee, *To Kill a Mockingbird*** — The Tom Robinson trial is the central conflict between Atticus (and a few others) and the entrenched racial structures of Maycomb. The injustice is not caused by one villain; it is produced by an entire community's assumptions.

2. **George Orwell, *1984* (1949)** — Winston Smith does not face a defeatable individual enemy. He faces the Party: a total surveillance apparatus that controls language, history, and thought. His every act of resistance is individual will against collective power.

3. **Nathaniel Hawthorne, *The Scarlet Letter* (1850)** — Hester Prynne is forced to wear the letter A and is ostracised by the entire Puritan community. Her struggle is a sustained opposition between individual dignity and collective moral enforcement.

**How to identify:**
- The protagonist's obstacle is a group's prejudice, an institution's rules, or a system of power — not a specific individual.
- Test: if the individual antagonist (if there is one) were removed, would the conflict still exist? Yes → Man vs. Society.

## Part 5: Satire, Parody, and Sarcasm

All three involve criticism or mockery, but they differ in target, scope, and purpose.

---

### Comparison Table

| | Satire | Parody | Sarcasm |
|---|---|---|---|
| Target | Real-world social phenomena, institutions, or human behaviour | A specific literary work or a genre's conventions | A specific person or their actions in the current moment |
| Scope | Usually a whole work or extended passage | Usually a whole work or extended passage | Usually a single remark or short exchange |
| Method | Exaggeration, irony, absurdity — with reformist intent | Exaggerating the stylistic features of the original to produce comic effect | Saying the opposite of what is meant, with an aggressive or mocking edge |
| Core purpose | Critique society; prompt reflection; intend change | Mock a literary form itself; comic effect is the primary goal | Diminish or humiliate the immediate target; no reformist intent |

---

### 5.1 Satire

**Definition:** The use of humour, exaggeration, absurdity, or irony to criticise social phenomena, political institutions, or widespread human folly — with an underlying intent to warn, reform, or prompt reflection.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Jonathan Swift, *A Modest Proposal* (1729)**
The narrator, in the register of a sober economic treatise, proposes that Irish infants be sold as food. Swift's actual target is British indifference to extreme poverty in Ireland and the colonial reduction of human beings to economic units. The form is rational; the content is monstrous; the gap is the satire.

**Example 2 — George Orwell, *Animal Farm* (1945)**
The farm animals overthrow their human owners and establish an "equal" society. The pigs gradually become the new tyrants. The novel uses the fable form to satirise Stalinist communism directly: the revolutionary slogan ("All animals are equal") is corrupted by power into "but some animals are more equal than others."

**Example 3 — Mark Twain, *Adventures of Huckleberry Finn* (1884)**
A boy fleeing "civilisation" narrates the novel through an apparently naive first-person voice. Twain uses Huck's incomplete understanding to expose the hypocrisy, violence, and moral corruption of the antebellum South. The "uncivilised" narrator sees more clearly than the supposedly respectable society around him.

**How to identify:**
- Ask: is the work's ultimate aim to criticise a real-world institution, social practice, or human behaviour pattern?
- Is there a serious intent behind the absurdity or humour?
- Yes → Satire.

---

### 5.2 Parody

**Definition:** A deliberate imitation of a specific work or a literary genre's conventions, in which those features are exaggerated to produce comic effect. The target is **a literary form itself** rather than social reality (though sometimes both are present).

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Miguel de Cervantes, *Don Quixote* (1605/1615)**
The novel imitates the language, narrative conventions, and heroic ideals of chivalric romance, but places the protagonist in a world where windmills are mistaken for giants and inns for castles. The collision between the romance's idealised conventions and mundane reality dismantles the internal logic of the entire genre. This is one of the earliest and most important examples of literary parody in Western literature.

**Example 2 — Jane Austen, *Northanger Abbey* (1817)**
The protagonist Catherine Morland, saturated in Gothic novels, reads an ordinary English country house as a Gothic castle of mystery and menace. She misinterprets ordinary people as villains. Austen systematically parodies the Gothic fiction of Ann Radcliffe, exposing the genre's conventions as formulaic and unrealistic through the gap between what Catherine expects and what is actually there.

**Key identifying feature of Parody:**
Parody requires the reader to be familiar with the "original" being imitated — the comic effect depends on recognising the gap between the imitation and the source. *Don Quixote*'s windmill scene is funny only if the reader understands what a genuine chivalric romance hero would do in that situation.

**How to identify:**
- Ask: is this work deliberately imitating the style or conventions of **another specific work or literary genre**?
- Is the imitation designed to create comic effect through the gap between the copy and the original, rather than sincerely continuing that tradition?
- Yes → Parody.
- Key distinction: Parody's target is **a literary form**; Satire's target is **social reality**.

---

### 5.3 Sarcasm

**Definition:** A form of verbal irony in which the speaker says the opposite of what they mean, with a clearly aggressive or contemptuous edge, directed at **a specific person or their immediate actions**. Sarcasm is a subset of verbal irony, but it is more deliberately wounding in intent.

**Examples:**

**Example 1 — Oscar Wilde, *The Importance of Being Earnest* (1895)**
Lady Bracknell, upon learning that Ernest Worthing was discovered as an infant in a handbag in a railway cloakroom:
> "To lose one parent, Mr. Worthing, may be regarded as a misfortune; to lose both looks like carelessness."

She uses the language of mild administrative criticism ("carelessness") to describe an infant's abandonment. The phrasing is cool and evaluative; the effect is brutal dismissiveness. This is one of the most quoted examples of sarcasm in English literature.

**Example 2 — Shakespeare, *Julius Caesar***
Mark Antony's repetition of "Brutus is an honourable man" is simultaneously verbal irony and sarcasm: it is irony because the literal meaning is the opposite of the intended meaning, and it is sarcasm because it is aimed as a direct attack on Brutus. This example illustrates that the two terms can apply to the same passage at the same time.

**Example 3 — Sarcasm in dramatic dialogue**
Sarcasm is most common in dramatic exchanges. The key signal: the speaker delivers apparent praise in a context that makes genuine praise impossible.
> A character has just done something obviously foolish. A bystander says: "Brilliant decision. Truly inspired."

**How to identify:**
- Phrasing that sounds like a compliment, but the context makes clear it is mockery → sarcasm.
- Distinguish from verbal irony: verbal irony does not require an aggressive intent (Austen's narrator is wry, not vicious); sarcasm must have the intent to wound or belittle.
- Distinguish from satire: satire criticises social phenomena broadly; sarcasm targets **this person, here, now**.

## Appendix: Quick Identification Decision Tree

```
When you encounter language that seems to say the wrong thing:

Step 1: Does the literal meaning of what the speaker says make sense in this context?
    → No / it is clearly absurd → some form of irony

    Step 2: Who does not know about the gap?
        → The reader knows; the character does not → Dramatic Irony
        → Everyone can see it; the speaker is saying the opposite of what they mean → Verbal Irony
            → Is there also a clearly aggressive intent toward a specific target? → Sarcasm
        → No one is "saying it wrong" — the outcome itself is the opposite of what was expected → Situational Irony


When you encounter a work that seems to be mocking something:

Step 1: What is being mocked?
    → A real social institution or pattern of human behaviour → Satire
    → Another literary work or genre's conventions → Parody
    → A specific person in the current conversation or scene → Sarcasm
```
